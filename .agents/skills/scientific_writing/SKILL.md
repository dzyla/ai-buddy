# Scientific Writing Guidelines

Use this skill when drafting, reviewing, or editing scientific manuscripts, grant proposals, literature reviews, or research reports. The primary objective is to produce writing that matches the standards of high-impact, peer-reviewed journals (such as Nature, Science, and the New England Journal of Medicine).

## 1. The Structure of Scientific Prose

High-impact scientific writing is built on cohesive, paragraph-driven narrative flow rather than fragmented lists or tabular summaries. Bullet points, numbered lists, and tables must be avoided because they disrupt reading flow, fail to capture the complex mechanical relationships between biological variables, and do not represent publishable academic literature. Authors must communicate all concepts, pathways, and findings using continuous, fully developed paragraphs. Each paragraph should begin with a strong topic sentence, followed by supporting evidence and mechanistic explanations, and conclude with a logical transition to the subsequent paragraph. 

Narrative cohesion relies on explicit transition phrases to link thoughts across sentences and paragraphs. Rather than presenting isolated facts, connect them using active analytical transitions such as "consequently," "in contrast to these findings," "consistent with this paradigm," "nevertheless," "furthermore," and "therefore." Emojis, colloquialisms, contractions, and conversational filler are strictly prohibited. The tone must remain objective, formal, and authoritative.

## 2. Organization of Manuscripts (IMRaD)

A standard scientific manuscript is organized into five main sections, each serving a distinct narrative purpose:

The **Introduction** establishes the context by introducing the subject area and defining the significance of the problem. It must review the historical and current literature to build a logical path toward the specific gap in knowledge that the study addresses. This section culminates in a clear, testable hypothesis or a statement of the study's primary objective.

The **Methods** section provides a detailed, reproducible narrative of the experimental design, model systems, protocols, reagents, and statistical analyses. It must be written in the past tense and structured chronologically, ensuring that a peer investigator could replicate the study exactly.

The **Results** section presents the findings in a logical, narrative sequence. It describes the data clearly and references figures or tables without interpreting their broader meaning or drawing premature conclusions. Statistical parameters, including specific p-values, confidence intervals, and sample sizes, must be integrated directly into the text.

The **Discussion** is the analytical core of the manuscript. It begins by summarizing the major findings, then interprets them in light of the initial hypothesis and the existing body of literature. This section must critically address any discrepancies, discuss potential confounding variables, acknowledge the limitations of the study, and propose concrete future directions.

The **Conclusion** synthesizes the main findings and discusses their broader biological, clinical, or physical implications, leaving the reader with a clear takeaway.

## 3. Structure of Grant Proposals

Grant proposals demand a highly persuasive, objective structure centered on feasibility and impact:

The **Specific Aims** page acts as a standalone blueprint for the entire proposal. It starts by outlining the long-term goal, the objective of the current application, and the central hypothesis. It then presents two to four testable, mechanistic aims. Each aim should be expressed as a heading (e.g., "Specific Aim 1: Elucidate the role of...") followed by a concise paragraph explaining the rationale, the proposed experimental approach, and the expected outcome.

The **Research Strategy** contains the Significance, Innovation, and Approach. The Significance section establishes the critical barrier to progress that the project addresses and how the scientific field will be advanced. The Innovation section explains how the proposal challenges existing paradigms or introduces novel technologies, concepts, or methods. The Approach section details the experimental design, preliminary data, feasibility, statistical power, and potential pitfalls along with alternative strategies.

## 4. Synthesis in Literature Reviews

Literature reviews must synthesize the current state of a field rather than merely summarizing papers chronologically or in a list-like fashion. The review must be organized under thematic headings that represent biochemical pathways, molecular mechanisms, clinical trials, or therapeutic modalities. 

Rather than stating "Author A found X, and Author B found Y," the text must integrate these findings to compare methodologies, resolve apparent contradictions, highlight gaps in the literature, and present unified mechanistic models. The review should conclude with a forward-looking perspective on unresolved questions and emerging therapeutic or diagnostic technologies.

## 5. Transformation: Bullet Points to Scientific Prose

### Substandard Format (Avoid)
> ### Pathogenesis of Myeloma Kidney
> * Myeloma cast nephropathy is the main cause of kidney injury.
> * Free light chains (FLCs) bind Tamm-Horsfall protein (THP) in the loop of Henle.
> * This forms solid casts that block the kidney tubules.
> * FLCs are also directly toxic to proximal tubule cells, causing:
>   - Oxidative stress (ROS)
>   - Activation of NF-κB and MAPK pathways
>   - Apoptosis via ASK1
>   - Tubular damage

### Preferred Scientific Format (Follow)
> ### Pathophysiological Mechanisms of Myeloma Cast Nephropathy
> Myeloma cast nephropathy represents the primary pathological driver of acute kidney injury in patients diagnosed with multiple myeloma. The central pathogenic mechanism involves the filtration of monoclonal free light chains (FLCs) through the glomerulus, which subsequently overwhelm the endocytic reabsorptive capacity of the proximal tubule. Upon entering the distal nephron, particularly the thick ascending limb of the loop of Henle, these FLCs bind specifically to uromodulin, also known as Tamm-Horsfall protein (THP). This interaction is mediated by the complementarity-determining region 3 (CDR3) of the light chain variable domain, forming insoluble, obstructing proteinaceous aggregates that fracture under hydrostatic pressure.
>
> In addition to physical intratubular obstruction, unfiltered and endocytosed FLCs exert direct cellular toxicity on the renal parenchyma. Megalin/cubilin-mediated endocytosis of FLCs within the proximal tubule triggers a cascade of intracellular stress responses. Specifically, FLC accumulation in lysosomes induces the generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS), leading to lipid peroxidation and mitochondrial dysfunction. Concurrently, FLC exposure stimulates the activation of the NF-κB and mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) pathways, driving the secretion of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as interleukin-6 and tumor necrosis factor-alpha. Prolonged intracellular FLC overload culminates in apoptosis, mediated by apoptosis signal-regulating kinase 1 (ASK1) activation and the subsequent recruitment of caspase-9 and caspase-3, which drives progressive tubular atrophy and interstitial fibrosis.
