# Scientific Writing Guidelines

Use this skill when drafting, reviewing, or editing scientific manuscripts, grant proposals, deep research reviews, or reports. The primary objective is to maintain maximum academic rigor, objective clarity, and professional depth.

## 1. Core Principles of Scientific Writing

- **Tone and Register**: Write in a formal, precise, and objective academic register. Avoid colloquialisms, contractions, hyperbole, and subjective language.
- **Strict Prohibition of Emojis**: Emojis must NEVER be used in scientific writing, reports, reviews, or academic summaries.
- **Avoid Bullet Points**: Do not use bullet points or numbered lists to describe narrative information, pathways, or summaries unless presenting discrete lists of items (e.g., specific aims or list of reagents) where bullet points are structurally required. Translate list-based facts into cohesive, logically structured paragraphs with explicit transition phrases.
- **Precision and Specificity**: Use precise scientific terminology instead of vague descriptions (e.g., use "pathogenesis," "etiology," "clonal proliferation," "uromodulin interaction" instead of "how it starts," "cause," "fast cell growth," "binding to protein").
- **Exhaustive Explanations**: Do not write short, shallow, or conversational summaries. Explain biological mechanisms, chemical interactions, mathematical derivations, or clinical implications in full detail.

## 2. Structure of Scientific Manuscripts (IMRaD)

When structuring or writing a full scientific manuscript, adhere to the standard IMRaD format:

1. **Title**: Concise, specific, and descriptive of the study's core finding or topic.
2. **Abstract**: A structured or unstructured summary of the study (~150-250 words) detailing the background, hypothesis, methods, principal results, and main conclusions.
3. **Introduction**: 
   - Define the research context and the significance of the problem.
   - Review existing literature to identify the specific gap in knowledge.
   - State the hypothesis, research question, or objective of the study.
4. **Methods**:
   - Provide a detailed, step-by-step description of experimental designs, protocols, model systems, materials, and statistical methods.
   - Ensure the section is sufficiently detailed to allow exact replication.
5. **Results**:
   - Present findings logically, referencing figures and tables without interpreting the data or drawing conclusions.
   - Describe statistical significance (e.g., p-values, confidence intervals) explicitly.
6. **Discussion**:
   - Interpret the findings in the context of the initial hypothesis and existing literature.
   - Address contradictions, anomalies, and potential confounding variables.
   - Outline the limitations of the study and propose future directions.
7. **Conclusions**:
   - Summarize the main takeaway and its broader scientific or clinical implications.
8. **References**:
   - Cite relevant, peer-reviewed primary literature using a consistent academic citation format.

## 3. Structure of Grant Proposals

For grant proposals (e.g., NIH-style research grants), organize the text around the following sections:

1. **Specific Aims**:
   - Establish the project's overall goal, central hypothesis, and clinical/scientific significance.
   - List 2 to 4 specific, testable aims (e.g., Aim 1, Aim 2). State the rationale, experimental approach, and expected outcome for each aim. Keep descriptions concise but scientifically rigorous.
2. **Research Strategy**:
   - **Significance**: Detail the importance of the problem or critical barrier to progress, how the project will improve scientific knowledge or technical capability, and how the field will change if the aims are achieved.
   - **Innovation**: Explain how the proposal challenges existing paradigms or employs novel concepts, approaches, methodologies, or instrumentation.
   - **Approach**:
     - Outline the experimental design, feasibility, preliminary data, and statistical analysis plans.
     - Discuss potential pitfalls and present concrete alternative strategies.

## 4. Structure of Literature Reviews (Deep Research / Reviews)

When compiling a literature review or deep research report, avoid simple chronological lists of papers. Organize the synthesis thematically:

1. **Introduction**: Define the scope, significance, clinical/scientific relevance, and historical context of the topic.
2. **Thematic Headings**: Divide the body into logical, mechanistic, or clinical sub-themes (e.g., cellular mechanisms, clinical trials, therapeutic interventions, pathophysiology).
3. **Synthesis and Critical Analysis**:
   - Compare and contrast different models or findings.
   - Highlight conflicts in literature and discuss possible reasons (e.g., methodology differences, statistical power).
   - Trace molecular or cellular pathways comprehensively.
4. **Future Directions and Clinical Perspectives**: Identify unresolved questions and upcoming technologies or therapeutics.
5. **Conclusion**: Summarize key conceptual shifts and takeaways.

## 5. Exemplar Transformation: bullet points to scientific prose

### Informal / Non-Scientific Version (Avoid)
> **Deep Research Report: Multiple Myeloma and Myeloma Kidney**
> 
> 1. Multiple Myeloma: Overview
> Multiple myeloma (MM) is a cancer of plasma cells in the bone marrow. 🧬 It accounts for 10% of hematological cancers.
> 
> * Median age is ~65-70 years.
> * More common in males.
> * 20-50% have kidney damage at diagnosis.

### Rigorous Scientific Version (Preferred)
> **Deep Research Report: Pathogenesis and Renal Manifestations of Multiple Myeloma**
>
> **1. Pathophysiology and Epidemiology of Multiple Myeloma**
> Multiple myeloma is a hematologic malignancy characterized by the clonal proliferation of terminally differentiated plasma cells within the bone marrow microenvironment, leading to the overproduction of monoclonal immunoglobulins or free light chains. Statistically, it accounts for approximately ten percent of all hematological malignancies, with a median age at diagnosis of sixty-five to seventy years and a higher incidence observed in male and Black populations. Renal impairment represents one of the most critical organ dysfunctions associated with multiple myeloma, presenting in twenty to fifty percent of patients at initial diagnosis and significantly correlating with increased mortality.
