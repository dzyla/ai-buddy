# Multiple Myeloma and Myeloma Kidney Pathogenesis: A Brief Research Report

## 1. Introduction

Multiple myeloma (MM) is a hematologic malignancy characterized by the clonal expansion of malignant plasma cells within the bone marrow, leading to monoclonal immunoglobulin or free light chain (FLC) overproduction, osteolytic bone disease, and multi-organ dysfunction. Despite substantial advances in therapeutic strategies—including proteasome inhibitors, immunomodulatory drugs, anti-CD38 monoclonal antibodies, and chimeric antigen receptor T-cell therapy—multiple myeloma remains an incurable disease with a median survival of approximately 6–8 years following diagnosis. Renal impairment constitutes one of the most frequent and clinically consequential complications, affecting up to 50% of patients at presentation and serving as an independent predictor of poor survival. The pathogenesis of myeloma kidney is multifactorial, encompassing direct tubular toxicity of monoclonal FLCs, formation of obstructing intratubular casts, immune-mediated tubulointerstitial inflammation, and progressive fibrotic remodeling of the renal parenchyma. This report synthesizes current knowledge regarding the molecular and cellular mechanisms underlying multiple myeloma pathogenesis and the specific pathobiology of myeloma-associated renal injury.

## 2. Pathogenesis of Multiple Myeloma

Multiple myeloma arises from the accumulation of genetic and epigenetic alterations in long-lived plasma cells that escape normal differentiation checkpoints and acquire self-renewal capacity. The disease evolves from asymptomatic premalignant conditions—monoclonal gammopathy of undetermined significance (MGUS) and smoldering multiple myeloma—through a stepwise acquisition of chromosomal aberrations including translocations involving the immunoglobulin heavy chain (IgH) locus at 14q32, hyperdiploidy, and deletions of chromosome arms 13q, 17p, and 1p. These genetic lesions activate oncogenic signaling cascades that drive unchecked proliferation, inhibit apoptosis, and confer resistance to microenvironmental growth signals.

Central to myeloma pathogenesis is the dysregulated activation of several key intracellular pathways. The NF-κB signaling axis, frequently activated through IκBα degradation or CD40 ligand-mediated stimulation, promotes plasma cell survival by upregulating anti-apoptotic proteins including BCL-2, BCL-xL, and MCL-1, while concurrently driving the expression of cytokines that sustain tumor growth. The PI3K/AKT pathway is activated by autocrine and paracrine signaling from the bone marrow niche, conferring resistance to both intrinsic and therapy-induced apoptotic stimuli through phosphorylation-dependent inactivation of pro-apoptotic mediators such as BAD and FOXO transcription factors. The JAK/STAT pathway, particularly STAT3 activation, is engaged by interleukin-6 (IL-6) and other myeloma-associated cytokines and contributes to both proliferation and immune evasion. Additionally, the unfolded protein response (UPR) and endoplasmic reticulum stress pathways are chronically engaged, as the massive synthesis of monoclonal immunoglobulin overwhelms the secretory capacity of the endoplasmic reticulum, necessitating adaptive responses mediated by IRE1α, PERK, and ATF6 that paradoxically support malignant cell survival.

The bone marrow microenvironment exerts an equally critical role in myeloma pathogenesis. Malignant plasma cells interact extensively with stromal cells, osteoblasts, osteoclasts, endothelial cells, and immune effector populations through a combination of cell adhesion molecules and soluble cytokine networks. Adhesion molecules including very late antigen-4 (VLA-4), intercellular adhesion molecule-1 (ICAM-1), and CD44 mediate tight binding of myeloma cells to extracellular matrix components and stromal surfaces, generating integrin-dependent survival signals that activate the NF-κB, PI3K/AKT, and focal adhesion kinase pathways. Cytokine production is profoundly dysregulated within the myeloma microenvironment. IL-6, basic fibroblast growth factor (bFGF), insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF-1), and vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) are produced by both myeloma cells and microenvironmental stromal elements, establishing an autocrine and paracrine growth loop that sustains clonal expansion.

Immune evasion represents another hallmark of myeloma pathogenesis. Myeloma cells exploit multiple mechanisms to subvert anti-tumor immune surveillance, including upregulation of programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1), secretion of immunosuppressive cytokines such as transforming growth factor-beta (TGF-β) and IL-10, and recruitment of myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) and regulatory T cells to the bone marrow niche. Notably, CD84, a member of the signaling lymphocyte activation molecule (SLAM) family, has been identified as a regulator of this immunosuppressive landscape. Myeloma cells secrete macrophage migration inhibitory factor (MIF), which induces CD84 expression on microenvironmental cells, thereby promoting differentiation of monocytic and granulocytic MDSCs and upregulating PD-L1 expression, collectively suppressing cytotoxic T cell function and enabling tumor persistence.

## 3. Myeloma Kidney: Pathobiological Mechanisms

### 3.1. Cast Nephropathy (Myeloma Cast Nephropathy)

Cast nephropathy is the most prevalent form of myeloma-associated renal injury and the primary cause of acute kidney injury in these patients. The pathogenic sequence begins with the glomerular filtration of excessively produced monoclonal FLCs, which are low-molecular-weight proteins (approximately 22–45 kDa) that freely traverse the glomerular filtration barrier. Upon entering the tubular lumen, particularly within the thick ascending limb of the loop of Henle and the early distal tubule, FLCs encounter uromodulin (Tamm-Horsfall protein), a high-molecular-weight glycoprotein exclusively synthesized by these epithelial cells and constituting the most abundant protein excreted under physiological conditions.

The interaction between FLCs and uromodulin is mediated by the complementarity-determining region 3 (CDR3) of the light chain variable domain, which binds with specificity to the epidermal growth factor-like domains of uromodulin. This binding event initiates polymerization and precipitation of FLC–uromodulin complexes into insoluble, eosinophilic, fractured casts that occlude the tubular lumen. The composition and structural properties of these casts vary depending on the biochemical characteristics of the involved light chain. Lambda-type FLCs predominate in cast nephropathy, with studies indicating that approximately 70–80% of cast nephropathy-associated FLCs are of the lambda isotype. These lambda FLCs exhibit characteristic resistance to proteolytic degradation and demonstrate specific reactivity with uromodulin that distinguishes them from FLCs associated with other tubulopathies such as Fanconi syndrome, where kappa-type FLCs typically predominate.

The obstructing casts incite a robust inflammatory and fibrotic response within the renal parenchyma. Tubular epithelial cells adjacent to cast material undergo apoptotic and necrotic cell death, accompanied by denudation of the tubular basement membrane and subsequent exposure of interstitial matrix components. Macrophage and lymphocytic infiltration of the interstitium generates a chronic inflammatory milieu characterized by elevated concentrations of tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α), IL-6, IL-8, and monocyte chemoattractant protein-1 (MCP-1), which collectively promote epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition (EMT) and activate fibroblasts. Over time, this process leads to progressive tubular atrophy and interstitial fibrosis, ultimately resulting in irreversible loss of renal function if the underlying plasma cell clone is not rapidly suppressed.

### 3.2. Direct Proximal Tubular Toxicity of Free Light Chains

Beyond cast formation, monoclonal FLCs exert direct cytotoxic effects on renal proximal tubular epithelial cells, constituting a second major mechanism of myeloma kidney injury. Under physiological conditions, filtered FLCs are reabsorbed by proximal tubular cells through receptor-mediated endocytosis mediated by the heteromeric receptor complex composed of megalin and cubilin. This process targets FLCs to lysosomal degradative pathways for proteolytic breakdown. In the setting of excessive FLC production characteristic of myeloma, the megalin/cubilin endocytic machinery becomes saturated, resulting in the intracellular accumulation of intact and partially degraded FLCs within lysosomes and the cytoplasmic compartment.

The intracellular processing of monoclonal FLCs generates reactive oxygen species (ROS), particularly hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂), as a byproduct of light chain catabolism. The generation of H₂O₂ is specifically mediated by the variable light chain (VL) domain and requires a critical tryptophan residue within this domain for optimal oxidant production. The consequent oxidative stress triggers activation of multiple intracellular signaling cascades within proximal tubular epithelial cells. Notably, H₂O₂-mediated signaling activates the JAK2/STAT1 pathway, which serves as a seminal event in the downstream generation of both pro-inflammatory and profibrotic mediators. STAT1 activation drives the production of interleukin-1β (IL-1β) through induction of caspase-1-dependent inflammasome activation, while simultaneously promoting the activation of TGF-β via αvβ6 integrin, a master regulator of fibrotic remodeling.

Concurrently, FLC accumulation within proximal tubular cells induces phosphorylation of mitogen-activated protein kinases, prominently p38 MAPK, alongside activation of nuclear transcription factors NF-κB and AP-1. These transcriptional regulators orchestrate the synthesis and secretion of pro-inflammatory cytokines including TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8, and MCP-1, establishing a self-amplifying inflammatory loop within the renal tubulointerstitium. The persistent inflammatory milieu drives epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition of proximal tubular cells, characterized by loss of apical-basal polarity, downregulation of epithelial markers including E-cadherin and citokeratin, and acquisition of mesenchymal phenotypes expressing fibroblast-specific protein-1 (FSP-1) and vimentin. EMT contributes directly to the progressive tubulointerstitial fibrosis that determines long-term renal prognosis.

FLC-induced oxidative stress also produces direct ultrastructural damage to proximal tubular cells, manifesting as mitochondrial swelling, lysosomal distension with myelin figures, cytoskeletal disarray, and disruption of the luminal brush border. Functional consequences include inhibition of active transport processes, resulting in Fanconi syndrome characterized by renal tubular acidosis, phosphaturia, aminoaciduria, and glucosuria—a manifestation particularly associated with kappa-type FLCs exhibiting particular resistance to cathepsin B digestion within lysosomes.

### 3.3. Other Patterns of Renal Involvement

Although cast nephropathy and direct FLC toxicity predominate, myeloma kidney encompasses a broader spectrum of renal lesions. Light chain deposition disease (LCDD) involves the deposition of non-fibrillar monoclonal FLC fragments along the glomerular and tubular basement membranes, driven by interactions between glomerulopathic FLCs and mesangial cells. These interactions trigger cytoskeletal rearrangements, PDGF-β receptor activation, nuclear translocation of c-fos and NF-κB, and production of MCP-1, collectively promoting mesangial proliferation and matrix expansion. Immunoglobulin light chain (AL) amyloidosis represents the least common but most histologically distinctive renal manifestation, with amyloidogenic FLCs assembling into cross-β-pleated sheet fibrils that deposit in glomerular, vascular, and tubular compartments, ultimately disrupting the filtration barrier and causing nephrotic syndrome. Rarely, co-occurrence of intratubular amyloidosis and cast nephropathy has been described, with unique amyloidogenic light chain casts forming under specific tubular fluid conditions, underscoring the pleiotropic pathogenic potential of monoclonal FLCs.

## 4. Clinical Implications and Therapeutic Perspectives

The molecular understanding of myeloma kidney pathogenesis has informed contemporary therapeutic strategies aimed at rapidly reducing the circulating FLC burden.(clone-directed therapy with bortezomib-based regimens combined with high-dose dexamethasone constitutes the reference standard for newly diagnosed myeloma patients with renal impairment. The addition of cyclophosphamide or lenalidomide may further improve hematological response, although tolerability considerations are paramount in this population. Emerging evidence supports the role of anti-CD38 monoclonal antibodies in this setting. Extracorporeal FLC removal strategies, including high-flux and high-cutoff hemodialysis membranes, have demonstrated the capacity to accelerate FLC clearance and facilitate renal recovery, with histological resolution of cast nephropathy observed within weeks of effective therapy.

The identification of FLC-mediated JAK2/STAT1 signaling, ROS-dependent inflammasome activation, and TGF-β-driven fibrogenesis as central pathogenic mechanisms has revealed novel therapeutic targets. Pharmacological inhibition of oxidative stress pathways, inflammasome components, or TGF-β signaling may represent adjunctive strategies to preserve renal function beyond clone-directed chemotherapy. Furthermore, uromodulin biology—particularly the dual secretion of polymerizing and non-polymerizing forms regulated by proteolytic processing—offers potential avenues for modulating cast formation through enhancement of the non-polymerizing basolateral uromodulin pool.

## 5. Conclusion

Myeloma kidney represents a complex, multifactorial renal complication of multiple myeloma driven by the excess production of monoclonal immunoglobulin free light chains. The pathogenesis encompasses glomerular filtration of FLCs followed by cast formation through uromodulin interaction in the distal nephron, direct proximal tubular cytotoxicity mediated by oxidative stress and inflammatory signaling cascades, and progressive interstitial fibrosis orchestrated by TGF-β and epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition. Understanding these molecular mechanisms has not only clarified the basis of renal impairment in myeloma but also identified actionable therapeutic targets that may improve renal outcomes for patients with this devastating complication.
