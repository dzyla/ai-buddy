# Multiple Myeloma and Myeloma Kidney Pathogenesis

## Research Report

---

## 1. Introduction

Multiple myeloma (MM) is a clonal plasma cell malignancy of the bone marrow, representing approximately 1.5% of all malignancies and 10% of hematologic cancers. It is characterized by the neoplastic proliferation of a single clone of plasma cells in the bone marrow, with consequent overproduction of monoclonal immunoglobulin (M-protein) or free light chains (FLCs). The disease is clinically defined by the **CRAB criteria** — **C**alcium elevation, **R**enal failure, **A**nemia, and **B**one lesions — alongside newer myeloma-defining events including ≥60% clonal bone marrow plasma cells, involved/uninvolved FLC ratio ≥100, and >1 focal lesion on MRI [Rajkumar, *Am J Hematol*, 2024].

**Myeloma kidney** (cast nephropathy) is the most common cause of severe renal failure in multiple myeloma, occurring in 20–50% of patients at diagnosis and up to 70% at some point during disease. It remains a major determinant of both morbidity and mortality [Wang et al., *Ann Hematol*, 2024].

## 2. Pathogenesis of Multiple Myeloma

### 2.1 Clonal Plasma Cell Expansion

Multiple myeloma arises from a single transformed B-cell that has undergone somatic hypermutation and class-switch recombination but has escaped normal apoptotic and differentiation pathways. The malignant plasma cell acquires multiple genetic lesions including:

- **Chromosomal translocations** involving the IgH locus on 14q32 (e.g., t(11;14), t(4;14), t(14;16), t(14;20)) that activate oncogenes such as *CCND1*, *FGFR3/MYCD*, *MAF*, and *HYD* [Rajkumar, 2024].
- **Somatic mutations** affecting the unfolded protein response (UPR) pathway — particularly mutations in *TRAF3*, *CYLD*, *NFKBIA*, and *FAM46C* — which promote NF-κB activation and plasma cell survival [Rajkumar, 2024].
- **Copy number alterations** including del(17p) (TP53), del(13q), gain(1q), and del(1p) that drive proliferation and drug resistance [Rajkumar, 2024].

### 2.2 The Bone Marrow Microenvironment

The clonal plasma cell expansion is sustained by reciprocal interactions with the bone marrow microenvironment. Key mechanisms include:

- **Osteoclast activation**: Malignant plasma cells secrete RANKL, IL-3, and VEGF, stimulating osteoclastogenesis while simultaneously suppressing osteoblast activity through DKK1 and sFRP-2, leading to the characteristic lytic bone lesions.
- **Immunosuppression**: The myeloma microenvironment is enriched in regulatory T cells, myeloid-derived suppressor cells, and M2 macrophages, which facilitate immune escape.
- **Angiogenesis**: Plasma cells and stromal cells produce VEGF and bFGF, promoting neovascularization that supports tumor growth.

### 2.3 Monoclonal Protein Pathogenesis

The neoplastic plasma cells secrete enormous quantities of either intact monoclonal immunoglobulin (IgG, IgA, or IgD) or, in approximately 20% of cases, free light chains alone (Bence Jones myeloma). Lambda light chains are produced more frequently than kappa (approximately 60:40 ratio) and are associated with higher rates of cast nephropathy due to their greater propensity to aggregate.

FLCs are small proteins (~22 kDa) that are freely filtered by the glomerulus and subsequently reabsorbed by proximal tubular cells, where they undergo catabolism. In myeloma, the massive overproduction overwhelms renal tubular reabsorptive capacity, resulting in **bloom-filter overload** — the primary driver of renal injury.

## 3. Myeloma Kidney: Pathogenesis and Mechanisms

### 3.1 Cast Nephropathy (Classic Myeloma Kidney)

Cast nephropathy is the hallmark renal lesion of multiple myeloma and results from the precipitation of monoclonal FLCs within the renal tubules.

#### 3.1.1 Cast Formation — The THP-FLC Interaction

The central event in cast nephropathy is the binding of FLCs to **Tamm-Horsfall protein (THP, also called uromodulin)**, which is secreted by cells of the thick ascending limb of the loop of Henle. The interaction occurs in the distal tubular lumen, where the concentrated FLC binds to THP via the **complementarity-determining region 3 (CDR3)** of the light chain variable domain [Sanders et al., *Am J Pathol*, 2001].

Key features of this interaction:

- THP provides the structural framework for cast formation. Without THP, cast nephropathy does not develop.
- The CDR3 region of both kappa and lambda FLCs interacts with THP, but lambda FLCs show greater avidity, explaining their higher association with renal disease [Sanders, *Am J Pathol*, 2001].
- The resulting protein complexes are insoluble at tubular fluid pH and concentrate, forming the characteristic eosinophilic, fractured casts seen on light microscopy.

#### 3.1.2 Direct Tubular Toxicity

Beyond physical obstruction, FLCs exert direct cytotoxic effects on renal tubular epithelial cells:

- **Proximal tubule injury**: FLCs are internalized by megalin/cubilin-mediated endocytosis in proximal tubular cells, where they accumulate in lysosomes, causing cellular vacuolation, desquamation, and loss of brush border [Sanders et al., *Kidney Int*, 1988].
- **Oxidative stress**: FLCs induce reactive oxygen species (ROS) generation in tubular epithelial cells, leading to lipid peroxidation, mitochondrial dysfunction, and DNA damage.
- **Inflammatory signaling**: FLCs activate the NF-κB and MAPK pathways in tubular cells, inducing pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6) and promoting epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition (EMT) [Batuman, *Adv Chronic Kidney Dis*, 2012].
- **Apoptosis**: FLCs activate the ASK1-mediated apoptotic pathway via caspase-9 and caspase-3 activation in proximal tubule cells [Ying et al., *Am J Pathol*, 2012].

#### 3.1.3 Obstructive and Inflammatory Cascade

The formed casts physically obstruct tubular flow, causing:

1. Increased intratubular pressure and decreased GFR.
2. Tubular rupture and backleak of filtrate.
3. Recruitment of macrophages and giant cells that surround and attempt to phagocytose the casts, generating a multinucleated giant cell reaction and interstitial inflammation.
4. Progressive tubular atrophy and interstitial fibrosis if the insult persists.

#### 3.1.4 THP-Independent Cast Nephropathy

A 2026 study by Wang et al. challenged the classical paradigm, demonstrating that approximately **40.6% of cast nephropathy cases are THP-negative** by immunohistochemistry [Wang et al., *Kidney Dis*, 2026]. These patients present with more severe acute kidney injury (AKI), greater dialysis dependence, and more marked tubular damage, suggesting the existence of a THP-independent pathogenic pathway that remains to be fully characterized.

### 3.2 Other Renal Lesions in Myeloma

While cast nephropathy is the most common renal lesion, myeloma can also cause renal failure through:

| Mechanism | Prevalence | Key Features |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| **Light chain deposition disease (LCDD)** | ~5–10% | Deposition of FLC fragments along basement membranes, particularly kappa type; granular IgG κ deposition |
| **AL Amyloidosis** | ~5–10% | Amyloid fibril deposition in mesangium and vessels; Congo red positive, apple-green birefringence |
| **Monoclonal immunoglobulin deposition disease (MIDD)** | <5% | Deposition of intact or fragmentary immunoglobulin along basement membranes |
| **Hypercalcemia-induced nephropathy** | Common | Direct tubular toxicity, vasoconstriction, and volume depletion |
| **Drug-induced nephrotoxicity** | Increasing | From bisphosphonates (foscarnet), bortezomib, and lenalidomide |
| **Tumor lysis syndrome** | Rare at presentation | Acute uric acid nephropathy from high tumor burden |
| **Plasma cell infiltration** | Rare | Direct infiltration of the interstitium by neoplastic plasma cells [Hanawa et al., *Clin Exp Nephrol*, 2011] |

## 4. Risk Factors for Renal Impairment in Myeloma

- **Lambda light chain isotype** (vs. kappa)
- **High serum free light chain levels** (particularly >2-fold upper limit of normal)
- **Elevated FLC concentration at diagnosis** (>2 g/L)
- **De novo myeloma** (vs. progression from MGUS/smoldering myeloma)
- **Older age** at diagnosis
- **Hypothesized protective role of THP polymorphisms** that reduce FLC-THP binding

## 5. Diagnosis of Myeloma Kidney

### 5.1 Clinical Assessment

- Serum creatinine, eGFR, and fractional excretion of sodium
- Serum and urine electrophoresis with immunofixation
- Serum free light chain assay (Bence Jones quantification)
- Hypercalcemia screening

### 5.2 Renal Biopsy

Kidney biopsy remains the gold standard for diagnosing cast nephropathy. Histological findings include:

- **Light microscopy**: Large, eosinophilic, fractured, angular casts within dilated tubules, surrounded by multinucleated giant cell reaction
- **Immunofluorescence**: Restrictive light chain staining (kappa or lambda) on cast material; THP staining (if performed)
- **Electron microscopy**: Rarely needed; may show crystalline inclusions in tubular cells

### 5.3 Urine Microscopy

Emerging data suggest that urine microscopy ("liquid kidney biopsy") can identify characteristic myeloma casts and crystalline light chain inclusions non-invasively, potentially reducing the need for renal biopsy [Luciano et al., 2014].

## 6. Therapeutic Strategies and Outcomes

### 6.1 Primary Treatment — Chemotherapy

The cornerstone of myeloma kidney management is **rapid reduction of serum FLC production** through cytoreductive chemotherapy:

- **Bortezomib-based regimens** (bortezomib + dexamethasone) are the standard of care for MM with renal impairment. Bortezomib achieves hematological responses within 1–2 weeks [Zannetti et al., *Am J Hematol*, 2015].
- **Carfilzomib** (second-generation proteasome inhibitor) offers potent myeloma cell kill with a potentially lower risk of neuropathy but carries a risk of vasoconstrictive renal injury [Wanchoo et al., 2015].
- **Daratumumab** (anti-CD38 monoclonal antibody) is increasingly used and rapidly depletes CD38-expressing plasma cells.
- **Autologous stem cell transplantation (ASCT)** may be considered in eligible patients with renal recovery.

### 6.2 Extracorporeal FLC Removal

- **High-cut-off hemodialysis (HCO-HD)**: Early observational studies reported 76% dialysis independence with bortezomib + HCO-HD [Zannetti et al., 2015]. However, the **EuLITE randomized trial** (Hutchison et al., 2019, *Lancet Haematol*) found no significant difference in dialysis independence at 90 days between HCO-HD (56%) and standard high-flux HD (51%), failing to support routine use [Hutchison et al., 2019].
- **Standard high-flux dialysis membranes** remove only a small fraction of FLCs due to molecular weight constraints.

### 6.3 Supportive Measures

- Aggressive intravenous hydration
- Correction of hypercalcemia (bisphosphonates with caution in renal failure; denosumab preferred)
- Alkalinization of urine (controversial; may promote FLC solubility)
- Avoidance of nephrotoxins (NSAIDs, IV contrast, aminoglycosides)

### 6.4 Outcomes

- Approximately **50% of patients with cast nephropathy** achieve renal recovery with timely chemotherapy.
- Approximately **10% remain dialysis-dependent** despite treatment.
- Persistent renal impairment at 6 months strongly predicts poor overall survival.
- The presence of extensive interstitial fibrosis and tubular atrophy on biopsy carries a worse prognosis [Try & Harel, *Bull Cancer*, 2023].

## 7. Emerging Therapies and Future Directions

1. **Targeted blockade of FLC-THP interaction**: A cyclized CDR3-THP competitor peptide demonstrated proof-of-concept efficacy in preventing cast formation *in vitro* and in a rodent model (Ying et al., 2012, *J Clin Invest*). Clinical translation remains pending.
2. **Anti-CD38 therapy**: Daratumumab and isatuximab enable rapid plasma cell depletion, potentially offering faster FLC clearance.
3. **Bispecific T-cell engagers (e.g., teclistamab)** and **CAR-T cell therapy (e.g., ciltacabtagene autoleucel)** are showing remarkable efficacy in heavily pretreated myeloma with renal impairment.
4. **THP-negative cast nephropathy**: The 2026 discovery that ~40% of cast nephropathy is THP-negative [Wang et al., 2026] raises questions about alternative pathogenic mechanisms and potential targets for intervention.
5. **Non-invasive diagnostics**: Urine microscopy for cast identification and emerging urinary biomarkers may reduce the need for renal biopsy.
6. **Kidney transplantation** in selected myeloma patients with cast nephropathy and long-term hematological response shows improved survival compared to continued dialysis [Try & Harel, 2023].

---

## 8. Summary

Multiple myeloma is a hematologic malignancy driven by clonal plasma cell expansion within the bone marrow microenvironment, with pathogenesis involving genetic alterations, osteoclast activation, and immunosuppression. The overproduction of monoclonal free light chains is the primary driver of myeloma kidney — the most common cause of severe renal failure in MM. Cast nephropathy results from the binding of FLCs to Tamm-Horsfall protein in the distal tubules, causing physical obstruction, direct tubular toxicity via oxidative stress and inflammatory signaling, and progressive interstitial fibrosis. While bortezomib-based chemotherapy remains the standard of care, emerging targeted therapies, extracorporeal FLC removal strategies, and novel diagnostic approaches offer hope for improved renal and overall outcomes in this challenging patient population.
